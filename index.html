Quantum Computing

Definition

Combines unique hardware and algorithms to process complicated problems faster than (but not necessarily better than) classical computers.

Qubits

Short for quantum bit. Basic unit of storing information and performing calculations in quantum computers.

Why It's Useful

Classical computers "brute force" problems and test solutions (one at a time) to find the correct one. Quantum computers first analyze the problem at hand, determine which solutions are most likely to be correct, and then test those possibilities first.

Superposition

Definition

A quantum particle can represent more than one possibility (can represent a 0 and 1 at the same time, but only returns one value based on the inputs). State of a qubit collapses after measurements/results are made.

Definition

A quantum state can collapse into a non-quantum state. The collapse of qubits into a non-quantum state allows quantum computers to interact with classical computers regarding their computations.

Interference

Definition

Entangled (related) qubits present information in a wave-like structure. Constructive interference occurs when many qubits peak/agree about a particular outcome. Destructive interference occurs when the probability of an outcome is unlikely.

Constructive Interference: Qubits' values align with each other

Why Quantum Computing Works

Cold Temperatures

Quantum computers require extremely cold temperatures to maintain quantum states and prevent decoherence, typically operating near absolute zero.

Faster Algorithms

Quantum algorithms can process certain types of problems exponentially faster than classical computers by leveraging quantum properties like superposition and entanglement.

High Investments

Companies like IBM have dedicated facilities for research and quantum processing so that the computers can operate under ideal conditions.

Limitations

Temperature Issues

Quantum computers require unsustainable temperatures near absolute zero, making them extremely expensive and energy-intensive to operate.

Error-Prone

Quantum systems are highly sensitive to environmental interference, leading to computational errors and unreliable results.

Decoherence

Unexpected decoherence can occur when quantum states collapse prematurely, disrupting calculations and causing system failures.

Promising Use Cases

Pharmaceuticals

Quantum computers can simulate molecular behavior and biochemical reactions to speed up research and development of medical treatments, potentially reducing drug discovery time from decades to years.

Chemistry

Quantum computers can find possible safe solutions for complex chemical processes in fields like material science to discover better alternatives to existing industrial methods.

Machine Learning

Helps to test the limits of existing machine learning systems and dramatically increases the speed of training complex models to promote faster growth.
